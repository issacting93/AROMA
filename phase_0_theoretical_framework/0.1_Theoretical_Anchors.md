# 0.1 Theoretical Anchors

AROMA is built upon five cross-validated theoretical pillars. Each anchor provides the structural vocabulary, methodological warrant, or procedural grounding for a specific dimension or annotation procedure of the taxonomy.

---

## Anchor 1: Cutrona & Suhr (1992)
**Source:** *Social Support Behavior Code (SSBC)*

**Inheritance:** **D1 — Support Type** (direct inheritance)

Cutrona & Suhr provide the foundational categories of need addressed in supportive interactions. AROMA inherits these 5 types directly to ensure compatibility with the social support literature:

| Support Type | Addresses |
|---|---|
| **Emotional** | Distress and the need to be heard |
| **Informational** | Knowledge gaps and uncertainty |
| **Esteem** | Self-worth and perceived competence |
| **Network** | Isolation and connection to communities |
| **Tangible** | Practical needs and concrete help |

*(Extension)* **Appraisal** — Addressing meaning-making, added from Lazarus & Folkman (1984) stress-and-coping literature.

**Why this anchor matters:** Without a principled vocabulary of support types, role definitions collapse into vague descriptions of "being helpful." Cutrona & Suhr give each role a dominant need category it addresses — making role activation conditions empirically identifiable.

---

## Anchor 2: Biddle (1986)
**Source:** *Recent developments in role theory. Annual Review of Sociology.*

**Inheritance:** **D2 — Care Role** (conceptual foundation)

Biddle provides the theoretical warrant for treating roles as analytically distinct from the functions and strategies that enact them:

- **Roles vs. Functions:** A role is a *stable relational stance* (identity-level); a function is a *discrete goal* pursued within that stance (action-level). AROMA's D2/D3 distinction maps directly onto this.
- **Invited roles:** Every role presupposes a complementary role in the interaction partner. An Advisor presupposes a Patient; a Listener presupposes a Witness-seeker. When the invited role is not accepted, the interaction fails.
- **Role expectations:** A role is defined by the obligations and expectations it places on the adopter — not by any individual utterance. A single utterance of advice does not make a system an Advisor; sustained authoritative stance does (see Blumer, below, for the enactment mechanism).

**Why this anchor matters:** Without Biddle, the claim that AI systems "adopt roles" is a metaphor, not a theoretical commitment. Biddle gives AROMA the vocabulary to define roles as a structural category with testable properties.

## Anchor 3: Blumer (1969) / Mead (1934)
**Source:** *Symbolic Interactionism* (Blumer, 1969), *Mind, Self and Society* (Mead, 1934)

**Inheritance:** **D2 — Care Role: annotation procedure** (enactment mechanism)

This is the most methodologically consequential anchor. Blumer's symbolic interactionism — building on Mead's concept of role-taking — provides the mechanism by which roles are enacted in interaction:

> **Role-taking:** The process by which an actor reads the other's expressed state, takes their perspective, and calibrates their own stance accordingly. A role is not declared; it is *performed turn-by-turn through this read-and-respond cycle.*

**Direct implication for AROMA's coding unit:**

A care role is not visible in a single utterance. It is visible *across a sequence of turns* as the AI reads the user's expressed need and adjusts its relational stance accordingly. This means:

1. The **unit of annotation** is a conversational sequence (minimum 3–5 turns), not an individual message.
2. A **role transition** is identifiable when the calibration pattern shifts — the AI begins reading the user's state through a different relational frame.
3. **Role-locking** (the failure mode AROMA documents in current systems) is the absence of this calibration — the system maintains a fixed stance regardless of what the user expresses.

**The D2 definition includes this explicitly:**
> *A care role is a stable relational stance that an AI agent adopts toward a user in distress, characterised by a distinctive core function, a dominant support type, and a characteristic set of linguistic strategies — enacted turn-by-turn through role-taking (reading the user's expressed need and calibrating stance accordingly) — and that invites a complementary human role in return.*

**Why this anchor matters:** Without Blumer, there is no principled answer to "what is the unit of analysis for coding care roles?" The annotation procedure would be arbitrary. With Blumer, the answer is grounded in a 90-year tradition of interactionist sociology.

## Anchor 4: Daft & Lengel (1986) / Bickmore & Picard (2005)
**Sources:**
- Daft, R. L., & Lengel, R. H. (1986). *Organizational information requirements, media richness and structural design. Management Science, 32(5), 554–571.*
- Bickmore, T., & Picard, R. (2005). *Establishing and maintaining long-term human-computer relationships. ACM TOCHI, 12(2), 293–327.*

**Inheritance:** **D5 — Interaction Modality** (structural constraint)

Daft & Lengel's media richness theory establishes that different communication channels vary in their capacity to convey the cues (immediacy, paralinguistic signals, feedback speed, language variety) that are required for different types of care:

- **Low-richness:** Text-based, asynchronous — suited to informational and esteem support where information density matters more than paralinguistic attunement.
- **High-richness:** Voice, video, embodied — required for emotional support where prosody, pacing, and non-verbal presence carry essential content.

Bickmore & Picard extend this to long-term AI relationships, demonstrating that *relational continuity* is itself a modality constraint — systems without persistent memory cannot sustain roles (like Companion) that require longitudinal attunement.

**Why this anchor matters:** D5 would otherwise be a list of platform features. Daft & Lengel/Bickmore establish it as a *structural constraint on role viability* — some roles cannot be instantiated in low-richness modalities. This makes D5 theoretically load-bearing, not decorative.

## Anchor 5: Nickerson, Varshney & Muntermann (2013)
**Source:** *A method for taxonomy development and its application in information systems. European Journal of Information Systems, 22(3), 336–359.*

**Inheritance:** **Methodological warrant for the taxonomy construction procedure**

Nickerson et al. provide the epistemological basis for claiming that AROMA is a *taxonomy* (not merely a typology or classification scheme). Their method specifies:

1. **Ending conditions:** A taxonomy is complete when (a) all objects can be classified into exactly one cell per dimension, (b) no new dimensions or characteristics emerge from new objects, and (c) each dimension is useful (non-trivially discriminates objects).
2. **Development modes:** Conceptual-to-empirical (top-down) and empirical-to-conceptual (bottom-up) — AROMA combines both: Phase 0 (top-down from theory) + Phase 1 (bottom-up from corpus).
3. **Distinction from classification:** A taxonomy adds theoretical grounding to a classification — AROMA's dimensions are not observed clusters, they are theoretically derived and then empirically validated.

**Why this anchor matters:** Without Nickerson et al., reviewers can ask "why is this a taxonomy and not just a list of roles?" The methodological warrant answers: the construction procedure follows a principled taxonomy development method with explicit ending conditions.

## Anchor Map

| Anchor | What it provides | AROMA dimension |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Cutrona & Suhr (1992) | 5 support type categories | **D1 — Support Type** (direct inheritance) |
| Biddle (1986) | Theoretical warrant: roles as distinct from functions | **D2 — Care Role** (conceptual foundation) |
| Blumer (1969) / Mead (1934) | Role-taking as the enactment mechanism; defines annotation unit | **D2 — annotation procedure** |
| Feng (2009) IMA | Sequential communicative moves as empirical precedent | **D4 — Support Strategy**; D2 extension |
| Daft & Lengel (1986) / Bickmore & Picard (2005) | Modality richness as structural constraint on role viability | **D5 — Interaction Modality** |
| Nickerson et al. (2013) | Taxonomy construction method; ending conditions; empirical warrant | **Methodological justification** |

> **Gate condition (from research protocol):** If any dimension cannot be mapped to a theoretical anchor in this table, that dimension is not ready for the taxonomy. All five dimensions are now anchored.

## Secondary anchors (used in argument, not dimension-defining)

| Source | Role in AROMA |
|---|---|
| Cutrona & Russell (1990) — Optimal Matching Model | Justifies role activation conditions: support effectiveness depends on *fit* between support type and stressor characteristics |
| Parsons (1951) — Sick Role | Grounds the Authority-Agency Paradox: authority implies the structural capacity to act on it |
| Rogers (1957) — Core therapeutic conditions | Anchors D3 Core Functions (empathy, unconditional positive regard, congruence) — especially for Listener and Companion roles |
| Feeney & Collins (2015) — Invisible support | Anchors D3 for Coach and Companion: effective support sometimes works best when not foregrounded |
| Sharma et al. (2020) — Empathy mechanisms | Anchors D4 Support Strategies in NLP-tractable communicative acts |
| Lazarus & Folkman (1984) | Grounds Appraisal as a sixth support type extension of D1 |
