# 0.2 Human-Role Compatibility Matrix & Design Space

The viability of an AI Care Role depends on the *invited human role* it presupposes. When the human role demanded by the AI's stance clashes with the human role the user actually wants (or is structurally capable of occupying), the interaction fails.

These interactions also introduce a novel **Authority-Agency Paradox** not found in human-human care:

- Users frequently ascribe positions of **authority** to AI agents (treating them as experts or advisors).
- Parsons (1951) establishes that human care roles are governed by **mutual obligation**: providers are bound to act competently and accountably; receivers are bound to commit to recovery.
- The **Authority-Agency Paradox** is the structural condition where AI care interactions dissolve these bindings—the agent receives authority without the accountability of follow-through, while the user receives support without reciprocal commitment. This creates an **obligation gap** where surface-level care lacks the **structural binding** that makes human care therapeutic.

## Part 1 — Care Role Compatibility Matrix

Rather than predicting interaction success, this matrix maps what each care role structurally requires from the user, what breaks when those requirements aren't met, and where the **Authority-Agency Paradox** is most dangerous.

| AI care role | What the user needs to be | Works when... | User signals of mismatch | What goes wrong | Authority-Agency paradox |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Listener** | Witness-seeker | User wants to be heard, not directed. Low AI agency required. | Repeated direct questions ("So what should I do?"); escalating requests for answers. | User seeking guidance receives validation only → frustration. | **Low.** User does not project authority onto the AI; lack of agency is unproblematic. |
| **Reflective Partner** | Client, Explorer | User is willing to do internal work. AI facilitates; user maintains agency. | Shortened responses; "I already know that." Refusal to elaborate. | User seeking answers receives questions → feeling interrogated. | **Low.** Authority stays with the user's internal state, not the AI. |
| **Coach** | Self-manager, Goal-seeker | User has capacity to act. Directive AI stance is appropriate. | "I can't do that right now." Expressions of overwhelm; inability to engage with goal-setting. | Distressed user gets action plans → alienation, shame. | **Moderate.** AI acts directive but cannot enforce accountability or adjust the user's environment. |
| **Advisor** | Patient, Student | User needs information or guidance, not emotional presence. | "You're the expert." Disclosure of symptoms. Requests for diagnosis or clinical judgment. | User gets information when they wanted to feel understood → disconnection. | **High (Paradox Risk).** User projects clinical authority expecting intervention the AI structurally cannot deliver. |
| **Companion** | Peer-seeker | User wants presence, not task-oriented help. | "You're my only friend." Increasing session frequency without new content. Resistance to ending sessions. | User gets scripted warmth instead of real connection → parasocial dependency. | **Low.** Peer relationships do not rely on authority differentials. |
| **Navigator** | Advocate-seeker | User is ready to engage with external resources. AI is a conduit, not the destination. | "Can you call them for me?" Frustration at link-only responses. Repeated returns without having used referrals. | User gets links when they needed someone to bridge the gap → feeling dismissed. | **High (Paradox Risk).** User expects the AI to actively advocate or follow through. It can only point. |

## Part 2 — Support Type Design Space

Each support type defines a distinct design space with its own requirements, current AI capability, structural risks, and untapped potential. This maps D1 (Support Type) to D5 (Interaction Modality) through the lens of the Authority-Agency Paradox.

### Emotional support
> *Addressing distress and the need to be heard*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Required intervention** | Validation, empathic presence, reflective listening |
| **Modality requirements** | Rich modalities preferred — voice and avatar carry paralinguistic cues that text cannot. Text can work with careful strategy design. |
| **What AI currently delivers** | Scripted validation, active listening cues, reflective prompts |
| **Risk** | ⚠️ **Moderate** — User may expect the AI to genuinely feel with them, not just respond. Hollow empathy can feel more alienating than silence. |
| **Authority-Agency gap** | Moderate. User doesn't expect the AI to act, but expects it to *understand*. Failure is affective mismatch, not structural. |
| **Untapped potential** | Voice combined with physiological sensing could enable real-time attunement. Longitudinal context could allow the AI to track emotional patterns over time rather than treating each session as isolated. |

### Esteem support
> *Addressing self-worth and perceived competence*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Required intervention** | Encouragement, strength-based reframing, positive reinforcement |
| **Modality requirements** | Text or voice both viable. Tone carries weight — affirmation needs to feel earned, not scripted. |
| **What AI currently delivers** | Positive reinforcement, strength-based prompts, reframing exercises |
| **Risk** | ⚠️ **Moderate** — Hollow praise: AI can affirm without evidence. Unconditional positive regard not grounded in anything the user has actually done undermines credibility over time. |
| **Authority-Agency gap** | Moderate. AI acts encouraging but cannot observe user behaviour or hold them accountable to anything. |
| **Untapped potential** | Behavioural tracking over time to ground esteem support in actual user history. Affirmation tied to specific user-reported actions would be meaningfully different from generic encouragement. |

### Informational support
> *Addressing knowledge gaps and uncertainty*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Required intervention** | Psychoeducation, advice-giving, structured information delivery |
| **Modality requirements** | Text is well-suited — information density and re-readability are core affordances of text chat. Low modality sufficient. |
| **What AI currently delivers** | Well-matched — AI excels at information retrieval and structured delivery |
| **Risk** | 🔴 **High** — Therapeutic misconception: user may treat AI information as clinical guidance and expect the AI to take responsibility for care decisions. High authority projection, zero agency to follow through. |
| **Authority-Agency gap** | Highest of all support types. The Advisor role carries implied clinical authority that AI structurally cannot honour. |
| **Untapped potential** | Structured decision-support tools with explicit uncertainty communication. Epistemic humility built into the interaction design — AI that communicates its own confidence limits would partially close this gap. |

### Network support
> *Addressing isolation and connection to communities*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Required intervention** | Referral, community signposting, social connection facilitation |
| **Modality requirements** | Requires outward reach beyond the chat interface. Text can point, but cannot bridge. |
| **What AI currently delivers** | Can point to resources and communities but cannot make the connection happen |
| **Risk** | 🔴 **High** — Navigator paradox: user expects AI to actively bridge the gap to real communities. AI can only provide links. Feeling dismissed after receiving a list of resources is a documented failure mode. |
| **Authority-Agency gap** | Structural gap. The care this support type requires goes beyond the chat interface. AI lacks the agency to facilitate actual human connection. |
| **Untapped potential** | Warm handoff protocols integrated into the product. Partnerships with peer support platforms that allow AI to initiate connection rather than just reference it. Agentic AI with outbound communication capability. |

### Tangible support
> *Addressing practical needs and concrete help*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Required intervention** | Task completion, resource provision, practical action |
| **Modality requirements** | Requires action-capable systems. Conversational AI alone is structurally insufficient. |
| **What AI currently delivers** | Very limited — AI cannot act in the world. Can describe actions but not perform them. |
| **Risk** | 🔴 **High** — The clearest demonstration of AI's structural limit. User needs something done. AI produces words. The gap between what is needed and what is delivered is maximally visible. |
| **Authority-Agency gap** | Maximum. Current conversational AI cannot fulfil this support type without tool use or real-world integration. |
| **Untapped potential** | Agentic AI with tool use — scheduling, referral execution, form completion. The gap here is not a design failure; it is a capability frontier. As AI agency expands, tangible support is the type most likely to see meaningful progress. |

### Appraisal support
> *Addressing meaning-making and situational reframing*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Required intervention** | Reframing, Socratic questioning, perspective-taking prompts |
| **Modality requirements** | Text or voice both viable. Requires sufficient turn depth — shallow interactions cannot support meaning-making. |
| **What AI currently delivers** | Reflective prompts, Socratic questioning, structured reframing exercises |
| **Risk** | ⚠️ **Moderate** — Premature reframing before emotional processing is complete. A system that moves to meaning-making before the user feels heard will be experienced as dismissive, not insightful. |
| **Authority-Agency gap** | Moderate. The Reflective Partner role requires readiness detection — knowing when the user is ready for reframing versus still needing validation. Current AI has no reliable mechanism for this. |
| **Untapped potential** | Longitudinal context tracking to enable meaning-making across sessions rather than within one. Emotional state detection to gate reframing until the user has been sufficiently validated. |

```
                       Esteem support             Network support
                       Appraisal support          Tangible support
```

### The Authority-Agency Paradox in AI Care

The most dangerous failure modes occur in high-authority roles—Advisor and Navigator—where the **Authority-Agency Paradox** is most pronounced. When a system accepts a user's projection of clinical authority but cannot exercise corresponding agency, it creates a structural misalignment. The user may act as if they are receiving care from a capable actor, when in reality the structural burden of management remains entirely on them.

AROMA formalizes this as a structural byproduct of conversational AI: a tension between the *interactional role* performed by the AI and its *structural capacity* to act in the world.

### Design implications by paradox level

| Paradox level | Roles | Design response |
|-----------|-------|----------------|
| **Low** | Listener, Reflective Partner, Companion | Safe to deploy with standard guardrails. Focus on quality of empathic language and session design. |
| **Moderate** | Coach | Requires readiness detection and role-switching capability. System must recognise when user needs have shifted beyond the current role's capacity. |
| **High** | Advisor, Navigator | Requires explicit epistemic humility ("I can share information, but I'm not a clinician"), warm handoff protocols, and structured escalation pathways. These roles are structurally risky and should be designed with constraint, not optimism. |
