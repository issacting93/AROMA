# 0.2 Human-Role Compatibility Matrix

The viability of an AI Care Role depends on the *invited human role* it presupposes. When the human role demanded by the AI's stance clashes with the human role the user actually wants (or is structurally capable of) to occupy, the interaction fails.

Furthermore, these interactions introduce a novel **Authority-Agency Paradox** not found in human-human care:
*   Users frequently ascribe positions of **authority** to the AI (e.g., treating it as an expert or advisor).
*   In human scenarios, this authority implies **agency**—the ability of the social actor to enact change in the situation or change the user's role.
*   By contrast, AI agents lack the agency to change the user's situation *and* the agency to change the user's role. This hollow authority creates unique failure modes when users expect the AI to act on the expertise they have projected onto it.

Below is the theoretical compatibility matrix predicting these dynamics.

| AI Care Role | Invited Human Role | Compatibility Prediction | Failure Mode When Mismatched | Authority-Agency Paradox Manifestation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Listener** | Witness-seeker, Help-seeker | **High.** Requires low AI agency. User maintains ownership of their situation. | User seeking guidance receives validation only $\rightarrow$ Frustration. | **Low risk.** The user does not project authority onto the AI, so the lack of agency is unproblematic. |
| **Reflective Partner** | Client, Explorer | **High.** AI facilitates internal work; user maintains agency. | User seeking answers receives questions $\rightarrow$ Feeling interrogated. | **Low risk.** Authority is shared or focused on the user's internal state. |
| **Coach** | Self-manager, Goal-seeker | **Moderate.** AI requires the user to have the capacity to act. | User in acute distress is pushed toward action $\rightarrow$ Alienation, shame. | **Moderate risk.** AI adopts an authoritative, directive stance but cannot enforce accountability or adjust the environment. |
| **Advisor** | Patient, Student, Help-seeker | **Variable.** Highly dependent on domain and risk level. | User seeking emotional presence receives sterile information $\rightarrow$ Disconnection. | **High risk (Therapeutic Misconception).** User projects clinical authority onto the AI, expecting it to take responsibility or intervene, which it cannot do. |
| **Companion** | Peer-seeker | **High.** Built on mutual presence rather than intervention. | User seeking specific help receives generic platitudes $\rightarrow$ Annoyance. | **Low risk.** Peer relationships do not rely on pronounced authority differentials. |
| **Navigator** | Advocate-seeker, Client | **Moderate.** AI is a conduit, not the destination. | User seeking immediate relief is given external links $\rightarrow$ Feeling dismissed. | **High risk.** User expects the AI (as an authority) to actively advocate for them or bridge the connection, but it lacks the agency to act outside the chat interface. |
| **Connector** | Community-seeker | **Moderate.** Requires user willingness to engage outwardly. | User avoiding social contact is pushed to connect $\rightarrow$ Withdrawal. | **Moderate risk.** AI suggests social solutions but cannot facilitate the actual human connection. |

## The Therapeutic Misconception in AI

The most dangerous failure modes occur in the high-authority roles (**Advisor**, **Navigator**). When a system accepts the user's projection of clinical authority but cannot exercise the corresponding agency, it invites **therapeutic misconception**—the user believes they are receiving treatment or being cared for by a capable actor, when in reality, the structural burden of care remains entirely on the user.
