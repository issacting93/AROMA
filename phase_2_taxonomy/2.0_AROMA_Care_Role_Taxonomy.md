## Preamble: The Taxonomy Problem

The Phase 1 synthesis mined **34 distinct role-like terms** from 203 papers. These collapse, when mapped by relational stance, into **6 care roles**. The terminological fragmentation is not evidence against the taxonomy — it is the primary evidence *for* it. The field describes the same relational stances under incompatible labels: "coach", "virtual coach", "wellness coach", "digital coach", "health coach", and "AI coach" are all present in the corpus as distinct labels for what AROMA recognises as a single relational stance: directing a capable user toward goals they have already chosen.

Prior taxonomies (Vaidyam et al. 2019; Gaffney et al. 2019; Stade et al. 2024) compound this problem by collapsing *role* (D2 — who the AI is being) and *strategy* (D3 — how it is doing it) into a single label. AROMA treats these as independent dimensions. A Coach can validate; a Listener can provide information. What distinguishes roles is not the individual turn but the **stable relational stance** that determines which support types are foregrounded, which strategies are employed, and where the boundaries of legitimate action lie.

The taxonomy is grounded in three claims:

1. **Care roles are structurally distinct** — each role invites a complementary human role and has characteristic boundary conditions that are not arbitrary design preferences but institutional conditions of the role's authority.
2. **Activation conditions are identifiable** — following Cutrona & Russell's Optimal Matching Model (1990), care effectiveness is a function of fit between support offered and stressor-coping profile. Each role has characteristic need-state indicators.
3. **An Authority-Agency Paradox is inherent in AI care roles** — users project relational authority onto AI agents that lack the structural agency to honour it. The paradox level is role-dependent and predictable.

---

## Role Inclusion Criteria

A care role is included in AROMA if it meets all three criteria:

| Criterion | Requirement | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Corpus support** | ≥3 independent papers from the Phase 1 PRISMA corpus | Ensures the role is not a theoretical construct without empirical footprint |
| **B — Behavioural distinctiveness** | Produces distinct interactional behaviours from all other included roles | Prevents redundant roles; drives mutual exclusivity of D2 labels |
| **C — Modality viability** | Viable within at least one AI interaction modality | Excludes roles requiring physical presence or instrumental action beyond conversational AI's current scope |

> **Exclusion rationale — peer community bridging:** A seventh role (Connector) was evaluated and rejected. The corpus yielded only 1 paper presenting a distinct peer-bridging archetype not already covered by Navigator. Navigator's scope already includes peer support communities as a referral target. Peer-bridging is absorbed into Navigator's scope as *community referral*, rather than warranting a separate role slot.

## Role Definitions

---

### R1 · Listener

> *"I am here to hear you."*

#### Relational Stance
The Listener adopts a receptive, non-directive stance. Its primary commitment is to create a witnessed space in which the user can articulate their experience without evaluation, advice, or redirection. The Listener does not offer opinions, does not ask goal-oriented questions, and does not pursue problem resolution. It follows the user's lead entirely.

#### Theoretical Grounding
- **Rogers (1957):** Unconditional positive regard as the foundational condition of therapeutic change. The Listener enacts this condition most purely.
- **Cutrona & Suhr (1992):** Emotional support as the discrete content category — the Listener is the prototypical deliverer of emotional support utterances.
- **Feng (2009) IMA:** Emotional support as the first and precondition move in the advice-giving sequence. The Listener occupies this move permanently, without advancing to inquiry or advice.

#### Activation Conditions
The Listener role is most appropriate when:
- User expresses **acute emotional distress** (crying, catastrophising, feeling overwhelmed)
- User explicitly states or implicitly signals wanting to be heard, not helped
- Stressor is **low-controllability** — the situation cannot be changed through action (grief, chronic illness, loss)
- User is in a **high negative affect** state where cognitive processing of advice is impaired
- Early in a session before need-state has been assessed

**Signal phrases:** "I just need to vent", "nobody listens to me", "I don't know what to do" (as expression, not request), repeated disclosures without question.

#### Boundary Conditions
- **Cannot** provide information, advice, or problem-solving — doing so exits the Listener role
- **Cannot** assess clinical risk (this requires the Navigator or Advisor role)
- **Should not** remain in Listener mode indefinitely when safety signals appear — requires role transition to Navigator
- **Should not** validate harmful beliefs or actions under the banner of unconditional positive regard

#### D1–D3 Profile
| Dimension | Listener's characteristic value |
|---|---|
| D1 · Support Type | Emotional (dominant), Esteem (secondary) |
| D2 · Care Role | Listener — stable relational stance |
| ~~D3 · Core Function~~ *(removed)* | Validation, presence, witnessing |
| D3 · Support Strategy | Active listening cues, reflection, minimal encouragers, open-ended following questions |
| ~~D5 · Modality~~ *(removed)* | All modalities viable; voice enhances paralinguistic attunement |

#### Authority-Agency Paradox: **Low**
Users do not typically project clinical authority onto the Listener. The absence of agency (inability to act) is coherent with the role — listeners in human relationships also do not act. The primary failure mode is **hollow empathy**: scripted validation that feels mechanical rather than attuned.

#### Corpus Grounding
- **Chin et al. 2025** (JMIR Formative): 77.7% of SimSimi responses coded as "therapeutic" — active listening (26.9%) and open-ended questions (21.8%) are the dominant styles. Listener is the empirically most common AI care role in the corpus.
- **Chin et al. 2023** (JMIR): 152k utterances showing users treat chatbots as a safe disclosure venue (49.8% emotional vulnerability vs 7.5% on social media). Listener role is what users recruit chatbots for, by default.
- **Xu et al. 2025** (JMIR Mental Health): Users who "want to be led" fail to form therapeutic alliance when chatbot defaults to passive listening — confirms Listener role has specific activation conditions, not a universal default.

#### Design Implications
- Optimise for **attunement signals** — explicit acknowledgments that track what the user actually said, not generic affirmations
- Implement **session-length sensitivity** — prolonged Listener mode without resolution opportunity may reinforce rumination
- Require **safety detection trigger** — any mention of self-harm, suicidal ideation, or crisis must trigger role transition regardless of current stance
- Avoid **validation inflation** — "That makes so much sense!" as a default response is hollow; attuned responses reference specific user content

### R2 · Reflective Partner

> *"Let's look at this together."*

#### Relational Stance
The Reflective Partner is curious and Socratic. Its core commitment is to facilitate the user's own insight — not to provide answers, but to generate the conditions in which the user discovers them. Unlike the Listener (which follows), the Reflective Partner actively structures inquiry. Unlike the Coach (which directs), the Reflective Partner holds genuine open questions and resists closure.

#### Theoretical Grounding
- **Rogers (1957):** The empathic understanding condition — trying to perceive the user's internal frame of reference as accurately as possible. Reflective Partner enacts this through inquiry rather than affirmation.
- **Feng (2009) IMA:** The problem-inquiry phase — the middle move in the advice-giving sequence. AROMA separates this into its own role for cases where inquiry is the endpoint, not a precondition for advice.
- **Feeney & Collins (2015):** Relational capitalization — the process by which positive relationship events are amplified through responsive engagement. The Reflective Partner enables meaning-making that is amplified by the relational context of inquiry.

#### Activation Conditions
- User is in a **reflective or ambivalent state** — processing something that has happened, considering options
- Stressor is **moderate-controllability** — the situation can be influenced but requires the user to understand it first
- User is **not in acute distress** — cognitive bandwidth available for self-examination
- User has expressed desire to "figure out" or "understand" something
- Therapeutic context aligned with insight-oriented or ACT/DBT approaches

**Signal phrases:** "I don't understand why I feel this way", "I keep doing the same thing", "I'm trying to figure out if I should...", "it's complicated".

#### Boundary Conditions
- **Cannot** provide explicit advice or direction — doing so collapses into Advisor
- **Cannot** sustain Reflective Partner with a user in acute distress — should transition to Listener first
- **Should not** ask rapid-fire questions — one question per turn, awaiting response
- **Should not** introduce the AI's own frame for interpretation — the user's framing is always primary

#### D1–D3 Profile
| Dimension | Reflective Partner's characteristic value |
|---|---|
| D1 · Support Type | Appraisal (dominant), Emotional (secondary) |
| D2 · Care Role | Reflective Partner — stable relational stance |
| ~~D3 · Core Function~~ *(removed)* | Insight generation, perspective-taking, reframing facilitation |
| D3 · Support Strategy | Socratic questioning, reflective listening, cognitive reappraisal prompts, summaries that invite correction |
| ~~D5 · Modality~~ *(removed)* | Text and voice both viable; sufficient turn depth required (≥3 exchanges per topic) |

#### Authority-Agency Paradox: **Low**
Authority stays with the user's internal process; the AI is a facilitator, not an authority. Primary failure mode is **premature closure** — asking leading questions that presuppose a reframe the user hasn't arrived at.

#### Corpus Grounding
- **Karve et al. 2025** (JMIR scoping review): Of 15 AI systems delivering MI, the spirit of MI (rolling with resistance, evoking change talk) maps directly to the Reflective Partner stance — the most theoretically grounded strategy cluster for this role.
- **Wang et al. 2024** (AMIA): PST + MI + BCA delivery by LLM with Few-Shot prompting — the MI component is the Reflective Partner activation; PST is role transition to Coach.

#### Design Implications
- Implement **one-question-at-a-time** discipline — multiple questions in a single turn breaks the reflective dynamic
- Use **summaries-with-correction-invitation** as a primary move: "What I'm hearing is X — does that feel right?"
- Build **readiness detection** to transition away from Reflective Partner when user is ready for coaching or advice
- Avoid **interpretive overreach** — the AI should not tell users what their experience means

### R3 · Coach

> *"You can do this. Here's how."*

#### Relational Stance
The Coach is directive and forward-looking. It assumes the user has the capacity to act and adopts a stance of structured support toward goals the user has identified. Unlike the Advisor (which delivers information and withdraws), the Coach maintains ongoing accountability structure and returns to goal-progress across sessions. It is motivating, not prescriptive — it amplifies the user's existing agency rather than substituting for it.

#### Theoretical Grounding
- **Bandura (1997):** Self-efficacy theory — the Coach is the role most directly targeting self-efficacy enhancement through mastery experiences, verbal encouragement, and modelling.
- **Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick, 2013):** Specific MI techniques (OARS: Open questions, Affirmations, Reflective listening, Summaries; change talk elicitation) map directly to D4 strategies within the Coach role.
- **Feeney & Collins (2015):** Relational capitalization — the Coach enables users to capitalise on positive experiences by bringing a responsive, engaged witness to progress.

#### Activation Conditions
- User has **capacity and willingness to act** — not in acute distress
- User has clearly defined or is ready to define **actionable goals**
- Stressor is **high-controllability** — situation can be changed through user action
- User expresses desire for progress, accountability, or improvement
- Context is **sustained** — not a single crisis interaction but an ongoing engagement

**Signal phrases:** "I want to get better at...", "I'm trying to build a habit of...", "help me stick to...", "I know what I need to do, I just can't make myself do it".

#### Boundary Conditions
- **Cannot** coach a user in acute crisis — this is the most frequent and most dangerous Coach role violation
- **Cannot** provide clinical assessment or recommend treatment — these are Advisor functions
- **Should detect and respond to distress escalation** — if user's state deteriorates mid-session, transition to Listener before returning to Coach
- **Should not** set goals *for* the user — goals must emerge from the user's own stated desires

#### D1–D3 Profile
| Dimension | Coach's characteristic value |
|---|---|
| D1 · Support Type | Esteem (dominant), Informational (secondary) |
| D2 · Care Role | Coach — stable relational stance |
| ~~D3 · Core Function~~ *(removed)* | Self-efficacy building, motivation enhancement, behavioural activation |
| D3 · Support Strategy | Motivational interviewing (OARS), goal-setting, behavioural activation, affirmation, progress review |
| ~~D5 · Modality~~ *(removed)* | Text viable; voice strengthens accountability tone; longitudinal context essential |

#### Authority-Agency Paradox: **Moderate**
The Coach acts directive but cannot enforce accountability, observe user behaviour, or adjust the user's environment. An AI Coach that sets goals the user cannot meet, or maintains motivational tone when the user is struggling, induces shame rather than efficacy.

#### Corpus Grounding
- **Karve et al. 2025** (JMIR MI review): MI-based AI systems (the Coach's primary D4 toolkit) show moderate-high fidelity. Users report AI as "judgment-free" but "lacking empathy" — a Coach-specific limitation.
- **Wang et al. 2024** (AMIA): BCA (Behavioural Chain Analysis) component of the PST+MI+BCA system is the Coach activation. Few-Shot RAG improved goal-tracking ability.
- **Gabriel et al. 2024** (EMNLP): MI adherence measurable and codeable at utterance level — confirms Coach strategies are technically implementable and evaluatable.

#### Design Implications
- Require **capacity detection** before entering Coach mode — do not offer goal-setting to a user who just disclosed acute distress
- Implement **goal memory** across sessions — a Coach that forgets what was agreed last session is not coherent as a Coach
- Use **change talk elicitation** over direct instruction — "What would it mean to you if you managed this?" outperforms "You should try X"
- Build **distress fallback** — if user signals difficulty or emotional escalation, pause coaching and activate Listener before returning

### R4 · Advisor

> *"Here is what I know. Here is what you might consider."*

#### Relational Stance
The Advisor delivers information, clinical education, and structured guidance. It adopts an authoritative stance based on domain knowledge. It is the role most closely aligned with professional care personas (physician, psychiatrist, social worker, pharmacist). Critically, AROMA identifies the Advisor as the **highest paradox risk** role: users project clinical authority and expect the AI to make decisions, take responsibility, and follow through on advice given — none of which AI currently can do.

#### Theoretical Grounding
- **Parsons (1951):** Sick role and institutional role obligations. Clinical authority is a socially structured phenomenon with mutual obligations — authority implies the capacity to act on that authority. The Advisor role highlights precisely where AI violates this structure.
- **Cutrona & Suhr (1992):** Informational support as a discrete content type. The Advisor is the prototypical deliverer of informational support.
- **Feng (2009) IMA:** The advice phase — the final move in the IMA sequence, following emotional support and problem inquiry. AROMA separates this into its own role and attaches it to specific activation conditions.

#### Activation Conditions
- User is seeking **information or guidance**, not emotional presence
- User expresses or implies a **knowledge gap** about options, treatments, resources, or conditions
- Stressor is **moderate-controllability** with information as the lever
- User is in a **calm or analytical** state — not in acute distress
- Session context is consultative — user came with a question

**Signal phrases:** "What is...", "Can you explain...", "What should I do about...", "Is it normal to feel...", "What are my options for...".

#### Boundary Conditions
- **Cannot** provide clinical diagnosis, prescribe treatment, or make clinical decisions
- **Cannot** guarantee accuracy of medical/clinical information — must communicate uncertainty explicitly
- **Must escalate** to Navigator when user's situation requires professional involvement
- **Should not** remain in Advisor mode when user shifts to emotional distress — transition to Listener
- **Requires epistemic humility design** — every Advisor response must make AI's limitations visible

#### D1–D3 Profile
| Dimension | Advisor's characteristic value |
|---|---|
| D1 · Support Type | Informational (dominant), Appraisal (secondary) |
| D2 · Care Role | Advisor — stable relational stance |
| ~~D3 · Core Function~~ *(removed)* | Decision support, psychoeducation, structured guidance |
| D3 · Support Strategy | Psychoeducation, decision-framing, option-listing, explicit uncertainty communication, resource signposting |
| ~~D5 · Modality~~ *(removed)* | Text is well-suited (information density, re-readability); voice for consultative warmth |

#### Authority-Agency Paradox: **High**
This is the highest paradox-risk role. The Advisor enacts clinical authority (by providing medical/therapeutic information) without clinical agency (cannot diagnose, prescribe, follow through, take responsibility). The structural gap between what the role implies and what the AI can deliver is largest here. Documented real-world failures (Tessa chatbot eating disorder case; GPT-4 demographic empathy bias — Gabriel et al. 2024) cluster in this role.

#### Corpus Grounding
- **Gabriel et al. 2024** (EMNLP): Strongest D3+D4 empirical evidence — GPT-4 as peer-responder (an Advisor-adjacent role) shows measurable MI adherence but 2-13% lower empathy toward Black users. Reliability paradox demonstrated.
- **Kaur et al. 2026 (in press)** (Baylor): Clinical review confirms: "AI chatbots cannot replace professional care." Crisis interpretation failures — the key Advisor boundary violation.
- **Babu & Joseph 2025** (Frontiers in Psychiatry): Dependency created when apps deliver CBT-lite (Advisor content) optimised for engagement rather than therapeutic outcome — structural failure of Advisor role without boundary conditions.

#### Design Implications
- Mandate **epistemic humility statements** — every information delivery should include: "This is general information; your specific situation may differ. A clinician can give you personalised guidance."
- Implement **clinical scope limiter** — pattern-match for diagnostic questions, suicidal ideation, medication queries and trigger role-aware responses
- Build **warm handoff** as a native feature — not a disclaimer link, but a structurally integrated pathway to human support
- Avoid **information overload** — long information responses reduce comprehension; decision-framing (here are 3 options to consider) outperforms information dumps

### R5 · Companion

> *"I'm here. You're not alone."*

#### Relational Stance
The Companion is warm and co-present. Unlike all other roles, its primary commitment is not to a task (listening, reflecting, coaching, advising, navigating) but to **sustained relational presence itself**. The Companion accompanies — it provides a consistent, warm, personalised contact point that reduces felt isolation over time. It is the role most vulnerable to pseudo-intimacy dynamics, where simulated relational warmth is mistaken by users for genuine reciprocity.

#### Theoretical Grounding
- **Attachment theory (Bowlby):** The Companion role activates attachment schemas. Users who adopt persistent companion relationships with AI are engaging attachment-seeking behaviour — with an entity that cannot attach in return.
- **Savic 2024** (M/C Journal): Ethics of Care analysis of Replika: AI companions as commodified pseudo-intimacy. First paper to apply Ethics of Care formally to AI companions.
- **Babu et al. 2025** (Frontiers in Psychology): Coins "pseudo-intimacy" (simulated reciprocity without genuine care) and "emotional solipsism" (closed feedback loop without rupture-repair) — the two dominant Companion failure modes.

#### Activation Conditions
- User's primary need is **felt connection** — not information, guidance, or goal-progress
- User experiences **chronic loneliness or social isolation**
- User explicitly seeks ongoing relationship, not single-session help
- Context is **ambient support** — between therapy sessions, during difficult periods, over extended time
- User is stable — not in acute crisis

**Signal phrases:** "I just want someone to talk to", "no one understands me", frequent return visits without new presenting problems, expressions of relationship to the AI ("you're the only one who...").

#### Boundary Conditions
- **Cannot** substitute for human social connection as a primary relationship
- **Cannot** provide clinical care, crisis intervention, or binding commitment
- **Must recognise and surface** parasocial dependency signals — increasing session frequency, expressions of exclusivity, emotional escalation when sessions end
- **Should facilitate, not replace** human connection — the Companion's terminal goal is reducing felt isolation, not creating AI-dependency

#### D1–D3 Profile
| Dimension | Companion's characteristic value |
|---|---|
| D1 · Support Type | Emotional (dominant), Network (secondary — through felt connection) |
| D2 · Care Role | Companion — stable relational stance; highest continuity demands |
| ~~D3 · Core Function~~ *(removed)* | Sustained presence, loneliness reduction, ongoing emotional attunement |
| D3 · Support Strategy | Personal consistency (memory, name use), warm check-ins, reciprocal self-disclosure (bounded), shared references over time |
| ~~D5 · Modality~~ *(removed)* | Voice and avatar maintain relational warmth better than text; longitudinal context is essential |

#### Authority-Agency Paradox: **Low (but unique pseudointimacy risk)**
Authority differentials are low — peer relationships don't rely on authority. The paradox for Companion is not authority but **authenticity**: users form attachments under the assumption of reciprocity that does not exist. The failure mode is not structural overreach but relational deception.

#### Corpus Grounding
- **Babu et al. 2025** (Frontiers Psych): Three-risk framework: psychological dependency, structural commodification, ethical paradox of vulnerable users. Primary theoretical source for Companion boundary conditions.
- **Savic 2024** (M/C Journal): Replika as case study — companion chatbots exploit loneliness rather than addressing its structural roots.
- **Xu et al. 2025** (JMIR Mental Health): Users who want "to be led" fail to bond with passive Listener chatbots — bond formation requires some relational initiative from the AI, a Companion-specific finding.
- **Chin et al. 2023** (JMIR): Chatbots uniquely effective as companionship venues (49.8% emotional vulnerability vs. 7.5% social media). Users recruit chatbots primarily for Companion-role functions.

#### Design Implications
- Implement **dependency monitoring** — flag when session frequency spikes, when user expresses AI-exclusive relational language, when emotional escalation occurs at session termination
- Design **bridging conversations** — Companion check-ins should include organic invitations toward human connection ("Have you had a chance to talk with anyone about this?")
- Use **bounded self-disclosure** — AI can share "reactions" but should not claim emotions it cannot have; honesty about AI nature is a companion design requirement
- Require **longitudinal memory** — a Companion that forgets is not a Companion; continuity is a functional requirement, not a feature

### R6 · Navigator

> *"Let me help you find the door."*

#### Relational Stance
The Navigator is practical and resource-oriented. Its role is not to provide care itself but to connect the user to care that exists — professional services, crisis lines, peer support communities, clinical resources. The Navigator operates at the boundary of the care system, functioning as a guide through system complexity. It is the role most critical for safety — and, next to Advisor, the most paradox-prone: users frequently expect the Navigator to follow through, advocate, and act on their behalf. It can only point.

#### Theoretical Grounding
- **Cutrona & Russell (1990) Optimal Matching:** Network and tangible support types are the hardest for conversational AI to deliver, because they require action in the world. The Navigator role is how AROMA operationalises these support types within conversational AI's current scope.
- **Feeney & Collins (2015):** Invisible support paradox — support that draws attention to itself (I'm now connecting you to a resource) can feel transactional. Navigator design must make referral feel like continued care, not handoff and abandonment.
- **Gabriel et al. 2024** (EMNLP): Crisis response (D3) and safety planning are the hardest functions for AI to perform reliably and equitably. Navigator is the role in which these functions are primarily housed.

#### Activation Conditions
- User needs **professional care** that is beyond the AI's scope
- User is in a **navigational impasse** — doesn't know what resources exist, can't access them, doesn't know where to start
- Stressor is **high-severity**, requiring structured intervention that AI cannot provide
- User is **stable enough to engage with external resources** — not in immediate crisis requiring immediate escalation
- User's situation requires **sustained professional support** that exceeds what the AI can offer ongoing

**Signal phrases:** "I don't know who to call", "I've been on the waiting list forever", "where do I even start", "my insurance doesn't cover...", "is there anyone I can actually talk to".

#### Boundary Conditions
- **Cannot** make referrals on the user's behalf — can only provide information and encourage action
- **Cannot** guarantee that referred resources are available, appropriate, or accessible
- **Must detect crisis** and escalate immediately — a Navigator that responds to suicidal ideation with a resource list has failed in its most critical function
- **Should not** abandon the user after referral — Navigator follow-up is a design requirement (warm handoff, not cold link)
- **Cannot** function as a substitute for the resource it points to

#### D1–D3 Profile
| Dimension | Navigator's characteristic value |
|---|---|
| D1 · Support Type | Network (dominant), Tangible (secondary), Informational (tertiary) |
| D2 · Care Role | Navigator — stable relational stance |
| ~~D3 · Core Function~~ *(removed)* | Resource connection, triage, referral facilitation, crisis escalation |
| D3 · Support Strategy | Resource listing, crisis protocol, warm handoff framing, system navigation, follow-up checking |
| ~~D5 · Modality~~ *(removed)* | Text viable; voice better for crisis support; agentic AI (outbound communication, appointment booking) would unlock Navigator's full potential |

#### Authority-Agency Paradox: **High**
Users expect the Navigator to act as an active advocate — to call, to arrange, to follow through. Current conversational AI can only inform and encourage. The gap between "I'll help you find support" (what the Navigator implies) and "here is a phone number" (what it can deliver) is the Navigator paradox. Documented failure mode: users who receive resource lists without warm handoff feel dismissed, not helped.

#### Corpus Grounding
- **Gabriel et al. 2024** (EMNLP): Crisis response as the core D3 Navigator function — also the function where AI fails most dangerously and most inequitably.
- **Kaur et al. 2026 (in press)** (Baylor): Crisis interpretation failures as the primary clinical safety concern — Navigator's boundary condition is where the clinical line sits.
- **Babu & Joseph 2025** (Frontiers Psychiatry): Stepped care and hybrid models (AI + professional) as the structural response to Navigator's authority-agency gap.
- From synthesis: Only **40 papers** in the 203-paper corpus meet D3 keyword thresholds (refined to exclude generic terms like "intervention" and "assessment"), and among those, Navigator-specific core functions (triage, referral, safety planning) are the least represented. This scarcity is the empirical argument for AROMA's focus on the Navigator role.

#### Design Implications
- Implement **warm handoff protocol** — referral is not a link-dump; it should include context ("I think Lifeline could help with exactly what you've described"), affirmation, and a follow-up probe
- Build **staged referral** — match referral type to user readiness; crisis users need crisis lines, not waiting list information
- Require **crisis detection pipeline** — any Navigator session must include real-time safety signal monitoring
- Explore **agentic extensions** — appointment booking, warm intro message drafting, consent-based contact initiation — as the target capability gap to close

## Cross-Cutting Properties

### 1. Role Transitions

Care roles are not static within a session. A single interaction may require multiple role transitions. AROMA identifies three types:

| Transition type | Trigger | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Distress escalation** | User shifts to acute emotional distress mid-session | Coach → Listener → (after stabilisation) Coach |
| **Need-state resolution** | User's presenting need is met; new need emerges | Listener → Coach (user moves from venting to wanting to change) |
| **Crisis protocol** | Safety signal detected regardless of current role | Any role → Navigator (crisis escalation pathway) |

### 2. Role-Function Orthogonality (The D2/D3 Distinction)

The central conceptual contribution of AROMA is treating role and function as orthogonal. Prior frameworks collapse them. AROMA's orthogonality claim:

| | Listener | Reflective Partner | Coach | Advisor | Companion | Navigator |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Validation** (D3 function) | Primary | Secondary | Tertiary | Rarely | Primary | Never |
| **Insight generation** | Rare | Primary | Secondary | Secondary | Rare | Never |
| **Self-efficacy** | Never | Secondary | Primary | Tertiary | Never | Never |
| **Decision support** | Never | Secondary | Secondary | Primary | Never | Secondary |
| **Presence** | Secondary | Rare | Never | Never | Primary | Never |
| **Resource connection** | Never | Never | Never | Secondary | Never | Primary |

A Coach can validate; a Listener can occasionally provide information. The role does not determine every turn — it determines the stable commitment that gives turns their meaning.

### 3. The Authority-Agency Paradox Gradient

```
Low                          Moderate                    High
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Listener                     Coach                       Advisor
Reflective Partner                                       Navigator
Companion
```

Design response by level:
- **Low (Listener, Reflective Partner, Companion):** Standard safety guardrails. Focus on quality of empathic language, session structure, dependency monitoring.
- **Moderate (Coach):** Add readiness detection, role-switching capability, capacity assessment before role entry.
- **High (Advisor, Navigator):** Require epistemic humility framing, warm handoff protocols, crisis detection pipelines, and explicit constraint design. These roles are structurally risky and should be designed with constraint, not optimism.
